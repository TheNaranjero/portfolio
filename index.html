Philosophy: My Approach to Data Science

🎯 Accessibility: Small Effort, Large Impact

I believe small design choices can greatly improve readability: using colorblind-friendly palettes, combining colors with shapes, selecting clear fonts designed for visual accessibility. Accessibility is not a constraint—it is a low-cost improvement that benefits all users.

📊 Visualization:Simplicity Before Complexity

A visualization should communicate an idea immediately. I prioritize simple, familiar graphs that can be understood by audiences without a technical background. If a chart requires a paragraph to explain how to read it, it is probably not the right chart. When more information is needed, I prefer multiple clear visualizations over a single overloaded figure.

📝 Communication: Plain Language, Powerful Insights

Most stakeholders do not need a statistical lecture—they need a reliable answer. I avoid unnecessary jargon and translate technical results into clear language while maintaining methodological rigor. Technical details matter, but they should never obscure the message.

🧩 Feature Engineering: Features Before Models

Complex models have their place, especially in areas like computer vision and generative AI. However, for most datasets, a well-designed feature can provide greater improvements than switching to the most sophisticated algorithm. I focus on understanding the data and creating meaningful features before trying to optimize model performance.

🌎 Context: Data Has a History

Data is not generated in a vacuum. Every dataset has people, places, methods, and decisions behind it. Understanding how the data was collected helps identify limitations and bias. At the same time, contextual information such as geography, demographics, time, or external events can become valuable sources of new features.

🔬 Diagnosis & Validation: Trust, but Verify

High accuracy alone does not guarantee a useful model. I value validation, diagnostic analysis, and testing assumptions to ensure that a model performs reliably beyond the training data. A model should not only fit the data—it should survive contact with the real world.

🚀 Beyond the Dataset: Think Outside the Box

Some of the best insights appear when we challenge the boundaries of the original dataset: integrating external sources, creating new representations, exploring alternative perspectives, and experimenting with unconventional ideas. Established methods provide a foundation, but curiosity is often what leads to innovation.
